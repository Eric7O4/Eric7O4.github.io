Weather Across Australian Capital Cities

Australia's seven capital cities span approximately 30 degrees of latitude, from Darwin in the tropical north to Hobart near the Southern Ocean. This visualisation draws on nine years of daily weather records covering maximum temperature, rainfall, humidity, and wind speed, and examines the differences between cities across these variables over time.

Temperature

Of all dimensions of weather, temperature has the most significant differences across Australia. Darwin records an average maximum of 32.5°C year-round with minimal seasonal variation, consistent with a tropical climate. Hobart averages just 17.9°C, a difference of nearly 15 degrees from Darwin. Perth and Brisbane record warm average temperatures driven by subtropical high-pressure systems. Sydney is moderated by its coastal position, while Adelaide's semi-inland location produces extreme summer maxima regularly exceeding 45°C alongside cooler Mediterranean winters. Melbourne's proximity to the Southern Ocean results in frequent cold-front passages and notable day-to-day temperature variability. The four charts below each address a different aspect of temperature: the bar chart captures each city's mean, the box plot shows distributional spread, the line chart traces monthly seasonal patterns, and the histogram shows the frequency distribution of daily readings across all seven cities. Together, they confirm that latitude is the primary driver of temperature differences between Australian capitals.

Average Maximum Temperature by Capital City (Bar Chart)

Darwin (32.5°C) records an average maximum more than 14°C above Hobart (17.9°C), the largest temperature gap between any two Australian capitals. The dashed line marks the national average across all seven cities. Four cities, Sydney, Adelaide, Perth, and Brisbane, fall within 4°C of each other in the low-to-mid 20s, forming a relatively close mid-range cluster. Melbourne sits just below the national average, reflecting the moderating influence of its southern latitude and proximity to the Southern Ocean. The bar chart provides the clearest single-number summary of each city's climate, and serves as a reference point for the more detailed distributional charts that follow.

Temperature Distribution by Capital City (Box Plot)

Each box summarises the range of daily maximum temperatures recorded across all years for that city. The centre line marks the median, the temperature that half of all days fall above and half fall below. The top and bottom edges of the box show the temperatures that bound the middle 50% of days. The lines extending above and below show the full range, excluding extreme outliers. Shorter boxes, such as Darwin's, indicate that temperatures remain consistent throughout the year. Adelaide's tall box reflects a wide spread, with recorded values exceeding 45°C in summer and approaching 10°C in winter.

Temperature Trends Over Time (Line Chart)

Darwin's near-horizontal trend reflects the limited seasonal variation of a tropical climate. Melbourne, Adelaide, and Hobart show pronounced winter minima. Hover over any month to compare all cities, or click a city in the legend to highlight it.

Distribution of Maximum Temperatures

The histogram displays the frequency distribution of daily maximum temperatures pooled across all seven cities. The majority of observations fall between 18°C and 28°C.

Rainfall & Humidity

Rainfall is the most unequally distributed of Australia's weather metrics. Darwin's monsoon concentrates nearly all annual rainfall into the six months from November through April, with the northwest monsoon delivering over 15 mm per day during the wet season and negligible amounts during the dry. Adelaide is the driest capital, receiving under 1.5 mm per day annually, following a Mediterranean pattern of wet winters and dry summers. Perth records a similar distribution, while Sydney, Brisbane, and Melbourne receive moderate year-round rainfall through east-coast troughs, subtropical disturbances, and cold fronts respectively. Afternoon humidity is among the most reliable single-day predictors of next-day rainfall. The scatter plot illustrates this relationship, with rainy-day observations concentrated above 70% afternoon humidity. The heatmap allows temperature and rainfall to be examined month by month, enabling identification of Darwin's wet-season peak, the winter rainfall maximum for Adelaide and Perth, and the relatively uniform year-round distribution across eastern seaboard cities.

Monthly Weather by Capital City (Heatmap)

The heatmap reveals distinct patterns depending on the metric selected. On temperature, Darwin's row is uniformly high-intensity across all twelve months, while Hobart consistently records the lowest values. Adelaide shows the greatest month-to-month variation, transitioning from peak values in January to its lowest in July. On rainfall, the pattern differs substantially: Darwin records high values during the wet season months and near-zero values from May to October, while southern cities maintain low, uniform values throughout the year. Use the Show dropdown to switch between temperature and rainfall views.

Humidity vs Temperature (Scatter Plot)

Each point represents one observation: afternoon humidity plotted against maximum temperature. Blue points indicate days preceding rainfall; grey points indicate dry days. The dashed trend line indicates a negative relationship between the two variables: as afternoon humidity increases, maximum temperature tends to decrease. This is consistent with the observation that high afternoon humidity is a reliable predictor of rainfall the following day.

Wind speed (x-axis), average daily rainfall (y-axis), and afternoon humidity (bubble size) are plotted simultaneously. Rainfall Over Time by Capital City (Stacked Area Chart)

Darwin's contribution increases substantially during the wet season (November to April) and falls to near zero in the dry season; the six wet months account for almost the entire annual total. Southern cities show the opposite pattern, with modest winter maxima and low summer values. Click a city in the legend to isolate it.

Seasonal Patterns & Rankings

Seasonal variation, defined as the difference between a city's warmest and coolest monthly averages, provides a more complete picture of climate than annual averages alone. Adelaide records the widest range of any capital: its January average maximum of 29.5°C falls to 13.9°C in July, a 15.6°C range attributable to its semi-inland position and limited oceanic moderation. Darwin records the narrowest range, with monthly averages varying by under 3°C throughout the year, consistent with a tropical climate that lacks a distinct cool season. Melbourne and Hobart both show substantial seasonal ranges due to their exposure to the Southern Ocean. The ranking chart presents temperatures in relative terms, identifying which city ranks highest each month rather than reporting absolute values. Perth ranks first in summer before Darwin assumes that position during the dry season. Adelaide falls from third in summer to sixth in winter. Hobart ranks last in every month, reflecting its consistently lower thermal baseline. The parallel coordinates chart combines all five metrics, enabling cross-dimensional comparisons across cities.

Multi-Metric Weather Comparison Across Capital Cities (Parallel Coordinates)

Each city is represented as a line crossing five normalised axes (0 = lowest, 1 = highest across all cities). Darwin records the highest values for temperature, rainfall, and rain days. Melbourne records the highest wind speed. Adelaide and Perth record the lowest rainfall values. Click a city to bring it to the foreground.

Seasonal Temperature Range by Capital City (Dumbbell Chart)

Blue markers indicate July averages; Red markers indicate January averages. Adelaide records the largest seasonal range (15.6°C); Darwin records the smallest (0.6°C). A greater bar length indicates a more seasonally variable climate.

Monthly Temperature Rankings by Capital City (Bump Chart)

Cities are ranked 1 (highest) to 7 (lowest) by average maximum temperature for each month. Perth holds the highest rank in January and February before Darwin assumes that position. Adelaide falls from third in summer to sixth in winter. Hobart ranks seventh in every month. Click a city to isolate its trajectory.

Geographic representation of weather data reveals spatial patterns that other chart types cannot adequately convey, including the temperature gradient from north to south, the lower rainfall of inland capitals relative to coastal cities, and the substantial differences in prevailing wind direction between cities. The interactive weather explorer allows any metric and month to be examined simultaneously, while the wind rose displays directional wind frequency for any selected city and year. Darwin's monsoon-driven wind pattern differs substantially from Sydney's predominantly north-easterly winds or Hobart's westerly dominance. The final two charts incorporate 2024 data to assess whether recent conditions represent a departure from the 2008 to 2017 baseline. Six of seven capitals recorded higher average maximum temperatures in 2024, with Sydney's December exceeding its historical average by 4.6°C. Perth was the only city to record a lower average. The slider map allows direct comparison between historical and 2024 values, with the magnitude of change visualised through colour and size transitions.

Conclusion

Analysis of nine years of daily weather records indicates that latitude and geographic position are the primary determinants of climate difference across Australia's capital cities. Darwin records an average maximum temperature 14.6°C above Hobart, and this north-south gradient is reflected in nearly all other variables. Darwin's monsoon produces a wet season average of approximately 5 mm per day, nearly three times Adelaide's annual rate, with the majority of its annual total concentrated in six months. Seasonal range provides further differentiation: Adelaide's January-to-July spread of 15.6°C identifies it as the most seasonally variable capital, while Darwin's range of 0.6°C is consistent with a tropical climate lacking a defined cool season. Melbourne records the highest wind speed of any capital despite relatively low rainfall, and Sydney records the highest afternoon humidity, which is the most reliable predictor of next-day rainfall across all cities. These findings indicate that no two Australian capitals share the same climate profile, and that proximity to the tropics and the ocean are the dominant factors shaping each city's weather characteristics.
