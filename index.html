Weather Across Australian Capital Cities, A look at climate change's effects.

Australia's seven capitals stretch across 30 degrees of latitude, from Darwin's tropical monsoons to Hobart's Southern Ocean winters. Nine years of daily weather records document how temperature, rainfall, humidity, and wind differ across cities, and how sharply 2024 departed from that baseline. The data tells a clear story: the climate is already in motion.

Temperature

Temperature is the most visible face of climate change. Darwin already averages 32.5°C year-round, nearly 15°C above Hobart. The more urgent story is the shift: six of seven capitals recorded higher temperatures in 2024 than their nine-year historical baseline. What were once record-breaking days are becoming routine. The charts below document each city's temperature profile: the averages, the spreads, the seasonal swings, and the daily distributions that reveal how far the baseline has already moved.

Average Maximum Temperature by Capital City (Bar Chart)

Darwin (32.5°C) records an average maximum more than 14°C above Hobart (17.9°C), the largest temperature gap between any two Australian capitals. The dashed line marks the national average across all seven cities. Four cities, Sydney, Adelaide, Perth, and Brisbane, fall within 4°C of each other in the low-to-mid 20s, forming a relatively close mid-range cluster. Melbourne sits just below the national average, reflecting the moderating influence of its southern latitude and proximity to the Southern Ocean. The bar chart provides the clearest single-number summary of each city's climate, and serves as a reference point for the more detailed distributional charts that follow.

Temperature Distribution by Capital City (Box Plot)

Each box summarises the range of daily maximum temperatures recorded across all years for that city. The centre line marks the median, the temperature that half of all days fall above and half fall below. The top and bottom edges of the box show the temperatures that bound the middle 50% of days. The lines extending above and below show the full range, excluding extreme outliers. Shorter boxes, such as Darwin's, indicate that temperatures remain consistent throughout the year. Adelaide's tall box reflects a wide spread, with recorded values exceeding 45°C in summer and approaching 10°C in winter.

Temperature Trends Over Time (Line Chart)

Darwin's near-horizontal trend reflects the limited seasonal variation of a tropical climate. Melbourne, Adelaide, and Hobart show pronounced winter minima. Hover over any month to compare all cities, or click a city in the legend to highlight it.

Distribution of Maximum Temperatures (Histogram)

The histogram displays the frequency distribution of daily maximum temperatures pooled across all seven cities. The majority of observations fall between 18°C and 28°C.

Rainfall & Humidity

Rainfall is where climate change cuts most unevenly. Darwin's monsoon delivers over 5 mm per day across its six-month wet season; Adelaide barely manages 1.5 mm all year. Climate projections warn of a future where wet regions get wetter and dry regions get drier, a divide already visible in 2024's data. High afternoon humidity is the strongest daily signal for next-day rain, a relationship that grows less predictable as temperatures keep rising.

Monthly Weather by Capital City (Heatmap)

The heatmap reveals distinct patterns depending on the metric selected. On temperature, Darwin's row is uniformly high-intensity across all twelve months, while Hobart consistently records the lowest values. Adelaide shows the greatest month-to-month variation, transitioning from peak values in January to its lowest in July. On rainfall, the pattern differs substantially: Darwin records high values during the wet season months and near-zero values from May to October, while southern cities maintain low, uniform values throughout the year. Humidity vs Temperature (Scatter Plot)

Each point represents one observation: afternoon humidity plotted against maximum temperature. Blue points indicate days preceding rainfall; grey points indicate dry days. The dashed trend line indicates a negative relationship between the two variables: as afternoon humidity increases, maximum temperature tends to decrease. This is consistent with the observation that high afternoon humidity is a reliable predictor of rainfall the following day.

Seasonal Patterns & Rankings

Seasonal range tells us how much a city's climate swings between extremes. Adelaide's 15.6°C spread between summer and winter is the widest of any capital, already a climate prone to heatwaves and cold snaps alike. A warming planet does not erase cool winters; it amplifies the contrast, pushing summer highs further from the baseline while winter lows remain. The charts below reveal how city rankings shift across the calendar and how each capital's seasonal character is becoming harder to predict.

Each city is represented as a line crossing five normalised axes (0 = lowest, 1 = highest across all cities). Darwin records the highest values for temperature, rainfall, and rain days. Melbourne records the highest wind speed. Adelaide and Perth record the lowest rainfall values. Click a city to bring it to the foreground.

Conclusion

Analysis of nine years of daily weather records indicates that latitude and geographic position are the primary determinants of climate difference across Australia's capital cities. Darwin records an average maximum temperature 14.6°C above Hobart, and this north-south gradient is reflected in nearly all other variables. Darwin's monsoon produces a wet season average of approximately 5 mm per day, nearly three times Adelaide's annual rate, with the majority of its annual total concentrated in six months. Seasonal range provides further differentiation: Adelaide's January-to-July spread of 15.6°C identifies it as the most seasonally variable capital, while Darwin's range of 0.6°C is consistent with a tropical climate lacking a defined cool season. Melbourne records the highest wind speed of any capital despite relatively low rainfall, and Sydney records the highest afternoon humidity, which is the most reliable predictor of next-day rainfall across all cities. These findings indicate that no two Australian capitals share the same climate profile, and that proximity to the tropics and the ocean are the dominant factors shaping each city's weather characteristics.
